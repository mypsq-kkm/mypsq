Development and Technical Validation of the Malaysia Patient Satisfaction Questionnaire (MyPSQ): Overcoming Systematic Error in the PSQ-18

Abu Bakar AM, Mohd Anuar E, Khalid KH, Sallehuddin MF, Adnan AS,

Clinical Surveillance Unit, Risk Management and Clinical Surveillance Section

Abstract

While the Ministry of Health (MoH) Malaysia adopted the Short-Form Patient Satisfaction Questionnaire (PSQ-18) in 2023, a subsequent data analysis (N=275,404) revealed critical practical vulnerabilities. Conclusion: The MyPSQ is a psychometrically sound and operationally efficient alternative to the PSQ-18. By re-engineering the instrument around direct-response items, it eliminates the systematic cognitive errors inherent in the PSQ-18 while maintaining domain-level fidelity, making it suitable for scalable national healthcare surveillance.

1. Introduction

Within Hospital and Special Medical Institution, Ministry of Health Malaysia, patient experience monitoring has evolved from legacy service quality frameworks towards digitally administered standardized instruments. The Patient Satisfaction Questionnaire-18 (PSQ-18; Marshall & Hays, 1994), derived from the original 68-item Patient Satisfaction Questionnaire (Ware et al., 1976), was formally adopted as the Medical Development Division digital patient satisfaction tool in 2023. While the PSQ-18 provides a globally validated psychometric foundation, a 2025 operational big data analysis encompassing 275,404 respondents exposed critical practical limitations in real-time digital deployment contexts. Performance metrics identified specific vulnerabilities in dimensions including "Time Spent with Officers" (77.49%) and "Access and Convenience" (77.68%). However, subsequent evaluation suggested these figures were substantially confounded by instrument-related measurement error rather than genuine clinical deficits (Abu Bakar et al., 2025, internal technical report).

The same report (Abu Bakar et al., 2025) identified that 66.7% of respondents reported cognitive difficulty with reverse-scored items, while 59.8% cited survey fatigue as a barrier to completion. The technical precision of the PSQ-18 is structurally compromised by negatively phrased items (e.g., "Doktor merawat saya dengan terburu-buru") paired with an inverse Likert scale in which '1' represents "Sangat Setuju" (Strongly Agree). This design creates a systematic cognitive misalignment that undermines data integrity across diverse patient demographics.

The rationale for reducing instrument dimensionality to mitigate respondent burden is supported by regional academic precedents. Notably, Imaninda and Azwar (2018) successfully adapted the PSQ-18 into a psychometrically robust 10-item Indonesian instrument (KKP-2017; α = 0.883), demonstrating that construct integrity can be preserved through rigorous item reduction. Building upon this rationale, the objective of the present study was to develop and validate the MyPSQ—a 7-item, direct-approach, multilingual instrument. Rather than positioning the MyPSQ as a dimensionally identical equivalent to the PSQ-18, this study sought to demonstrate that it constitutes a structurally superior instrument that preserves construct alignment at the domain level while eliminating the systematic measurement error endemic to the current implementation.

2. Methods

2.1 Study Design Framework

The validation protocol was structured across three sequential development phases to ensure comprehensive psychometric integrity:

  • Phase I (Item Refinement): Expert-driven content development focusing on direct-response re-engineering of 7 core items to eliminate double-barrelled and negatively phrased questions.
  • Phase II (Psychometric Validation): Cross-sectional concurrent validity testing between the MyPSQ and the Malay-language PSQ-18 (Hussin, 2016).
  • Phase III (Linguistic and Cultural Adaptation): Cross-cultural adaptation for the Malaysian multilingual context (English, Mandarin, Tamil, Iban, Kadazan) utilizing bilingual expert panels and Content Validity Indexing (Lynn, 1986; Polit & Beck, 2006).

2.2 Participants and Sampling

Data for Phase II were collected via an online questionnaire hosted on the CaPRiSK portal (Medical Development Division, Ministry of Health Malaysia). Sample size requirements were determined by the most stringent of three concurrent psychometric objectives. For internal consistency estimation, a minimum of 150–300 participants is recommended to obtain stable Cronbach's alpha estimates (Bonett, 2002). For concurrent validity, given the ordinal data structure and anticipated moderate-to-strong Spearman correlations (ρ = 0.4–0.7 based on the Imaninda & Azwar, 2018 precedent), power analysis following Zar (1999) indicated that approximately 85–150 participants are required to achieve 80% statistical power (two-tailed α = .05). Cohen (1988) was retained for the Bland–Altman sample size rationale (minimum 100–200 observations), which does not depend on the correlation metric.

To satisfy all constraints simultaneously across the 7-domain structure, a minimum of 150 participants was established. Accounting for an anticipated 10% rate of incomplete or invalid responses, a recruitment target of approximately 165 participants was set. 2.3 Statistical Analysis

Because both instruments employ 5-point Likert scales yielding ordinal response data, non-parametric Spearman's rank-order correlation (ρ) was used to assess monotonic concurrent validity between corresponding domains (Jamieson, 2004). Strength of association was interpreted using the classification framework proposed by Schober, Boer, and Schwarte (2018), which is calibrated for clinical measurement contexts: negligible (ρ < .10), weak (.10–.39), moderate (.40–.69), strong (.70–.89), and very strong (≥ .90). Internal consistency was quantified using Cronbach's alpha alongside a 95% confidence interval computed via the method of Feldt, Woodruff, and Salih (1987), which uses an F-distribution and is appropriate for fixed-k scales such as the 7-item MyPSQ. Bland–Altman analysis was conducted on normalized global scores to assess absolute agreement, reporting mean difference (bias), limits of agreement (LoA; ±1.96 SD), and proportional bias via regression of differences on means (Bland & Altman, 1986).

3.4 Phase III: Content Validity Index (CVI) Analysis

To evaluate the linguistic and conceptual equivalence of translated items across diverse Malaysian demographics, a Content Validity Index (CVI) approach was planned to be employed following established guidelines (Lynn, 1986; Polit & Beck, 2006). We are planning to have bilingual expert review panel (n = 3 experts per language) to evaluated all 42 items of the translation drafts across five target languages: English, Mandarin, Tamil, Iban, and Kadazan. Currently, we only manage to do a complete CVI for English, Mandarin dan Tamil, however, we are still getting the experts for Iban and Kadazan language. The next phase of this study will conclude Phase 3 and repeat CVI process as needed for translations that requires revision.

Experts rated each item on a 4-point scale for Conceptual Equivalence. Items rated 3 or 4 by a rater were classified as relevant; Item-level CVI (I-CVI) was computed as the proportion of raters rating the item as relevant. Given the small panel size per language (n = 3), the criterion for immediate acceptance without revision was set at I-CVI = 1.00 (i.e., unanimous consensus), consistent with the recommendation of Polit and Beck (2006). 4. Discussion

This study demonstrates that the MyPSQ provides a psychometrically sound alternative to the PSQ-18 for population-level patient satisfaction surveillance within the Malaysian healthcare system. The strong Cronbach's alpha observed should be interpreted with caution in the context of a multidimensional instrument: rather than indicating item redundancy, this value reflects the influence of a strong higher-order satisfaction construct that co-varies across all seven domains simultaneously—a finding consistent with the theoretical structure of global service quality models.

The observed moderate correlation in the Financial Aspects domain is contextually interpretable. The heavily subsidized nature of Malaysian public healthcare (Institute for Public Health, 2024) produces an expected ceiling effect for financial satisfaction items, limiting score variance and thereby attenuating the observed correlation coefficient. This dimensional sensitivity may warrant contextual recalibration in future instrument iterations.

The Bland–Altman analysis supports the practical utility of the MyPSQ as an aggregate-level surveillance tool. The absence of proportional bias across the range of satisfaction scores indicates that instrument discrepancies are consistent and do not systematically inflate or deflate scores for either highly satisfied or dissatisfied patients—a property essential for fair population-level benchmarking.

5. Conclusion

The MyPSQ represents a valid, reliable, and operationally efficient structural alternative to the PSQ-18. By substantially reducing respondent burden while maintaining robust domain-level construct fidelity, the instrument is well suited for real-time national healthcare surveillance systems. Its direct-response design eliminates the cognitive error associated with reverse-scored and negatively phrased items, enhancing data quality and usability across diverse patient populations, and ultimately supporting more accurate and inclusive patient experience measurement within the Ministry of Health Malaysia.
